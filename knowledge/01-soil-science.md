# Soil Science for Gardens

## Soil Types & Texture

Soil is classified by particle size into three components:

| Component | Particle Size   | Properties                                      |
|-----------|----------------|--------------------------------------------------|
| Sand      | 0.05-2.0 mm    | Excellent drainage, low nutrient retention, warms fast |
| Silt      | 0.002-0.05 mm  | Moderate drainage, moderate nutrients            |
| Clay      | < 0.002 mm     | Poor drainage, high nutrient retention, slow to warm |

The USDA soil texture triangle classifies soil into 12 types based on % sand, silt, and clay. **Loam** (roughly equal parts) is ideal for most garden plants.

### Quick Texture Test (Jar Test)
1. Fill a quart jar 1/3 with soil, add water to near top, shake vigorously
2. Let settle 24 hours — sand settles first (1 min), then silt (2 hrs), then clay (24 hrs)
3. Measure each layer to calculate percentages

## Minnesota Soil Types

Minnesota has 7 of the 12 global soil orders:

| Soil Order  | % of State | Characteristics                    | Gardening Impact                    |
|-------------|-----------|-------------------------------------|--------------------------------------|
| Mollisols   | 32.1%     | Thick dark topsoil, high fertility  | Excellent for gardens, prairie soils |
| Alfisols    | 27.4%     | Forest soils, clay-rich subsoil     | Good fertility, may compact         |
| Entisols    | 18.4%     | Sandy, poorly developed             | Good drainage, low fertility        |
| Inceptisols | 9.4%      | Moderate development, rocky         | Variable, often acidic              |
| Histosols   | 5.3%      | Organic/peat soils, wetland origin  | Very high organic matter, wet       |
| Vertisols   | 1.2%      | Heavy clay (>30%), shrink-swell     | Fertile but difficult to work       |
| Spodosols   | 0.2%      | Sandy, acidic, NE Minnesota         | Low fertility, very acidic          |

**Most metro-area gardens sit on Mollisols or Alfisols** — generally fertile but often clay-heavy, alkaline (pH 7.0-8.0), and slow to warm in spring.

**Source:** [UMN Extension - Soil Orders in Minnesota](https://extension.umn.edu/soil-management-and-health/soil-orders-and-suborders-minnesota)

## Soil pH

| pH Range | Classification | Common in MN?       | Impact                           |
|----------|---------------|----------------------|----------------------------------|
| < 5.5    | Strongly acid  | NE Minnesota         | Nutrient lockout, Al toxicity    |
| 5.5-6.0  | Moderately acid| Some forested areas  | Good for blueberries, potatoes   |
| 6.0-6.5  | Slightly acid  | Ideal target range   | Optimal nutrient availability    |
| 6.5-7.0  | Neutral        | Common in MN         | Good for most vegetables         |
| 7.0-7.5  | Slightly alkaline | Very common in MN | Most crops OK, iron can lock out |
| 7.5-8.5  | Moderately alkaline | Western MN       | Need sulfur amendments           |

**Ideal vegetable garden pH: 6.0-7.0** (6.2-6.8 optimal)

### pH Adjustment Rates

**To LOWER pH (alkaline → neutral):**
- Elemental sulfur: 1-2 lbs per 100 sq ft to lower pH by ~1 unit in loam
- Sandy soil needs less (0.5-1 lb), clay soil needs more (1.5-2.5 lbs)
- Apply in spring; takes months for microbial oxidation to work
- Max single application: 5-10 lbs per 1,000 sq ft

**To RAISE pH (acidic → neutral):**
- Ground limestone: 5-10 lbs per 100 sq ft to raise pH by ~1 unit in loam
- Sandy soil needs less, clay soil needs more
- Max single application: 50 lbs per 1,000 sq ft
- Takes 2-3 months to fully react

**Source:** [UMN Extension - Lime Needs in Minnesota](https://extension.umn.edu/liming/lime-needs-minnesota)

## Soil Testing

### What to Test
The UMN **Regular Soil Test** provides:
- Estimated soil texture
- Organic matter % (target: >3%, low if below 3%)
- pH level
- Phosphorus (target: ~20 ppm for vegetables)
- Potassium

Optional add-ons: soluble salts, lead, trace elements (Ca, Mg, Zn, Fe, Cu, Mn, B), nitrate.

### How to Sample
1. Collect 4-6 subsamples per garden area
2. Dig 6-8 inches deep with shovel or soil probe
3. Mix subsamples in a clean bucket
4. Air-dry wet soil before submitting
5. Submit ~2 cups of mixed soil

### When to Test
- Every 3-5 years
- Best timing: before spring planting or after fall harvest
- Always test when converting lawn to garden bed

**Source:** [UMN Extension - Soil Testing](https://extension.umn.edu/managing-soil-and-nutrients/soil-testing-lawns-and-gardens)

### Test Labs
- **UMN Soil Testing Lab** — primary recommendation for MN gardens
- County Extension offices provide free sample bags and forms
- Results returned in ~2 weeks

## Soil Amendments

### Compost
- Incorporate 1-2 inches of compost, 6-8 inches deep annually
- Improves structure in clay soil (increases air/water movement)
- Improves water retention in sandy soil
- Adds organic matter and slow-release nutrients
- WARNING: Over-application can cause excessive phosphorus and salt buildup

### Common Amendments

| Amendment          | Purpose                    | Rate                        | Notes                     |
|--------------------|----------------------------|-----------------------------|---------------------------|
| Compost            | Structure, OM, fertility   | 1-2" tilled in 6-8" deep   | Annual application        |
| Aged manure        | Nitrogen, OM               | 1-2" tilled in             | Apply fall, not on edibles|
| Peat moss          | Lower pH, improve drainage | 2-3" mixed in              | Acidic (pH 3.5-4.5)      |
| Perlite            | Improve drainage           | 10-20% by volume           | For raised beds/containers|
| Vermiculite        | Improve moisture retention | 10-20% by volume           | For raised beds/containers|
| Gypsum             | Break up clay, add calcium | 40 lbs per 1,000 sq ft     | Does not change pH        |
| Elemental sulfur   | Lower pH                   | 1-2 lbs per 100 sq ft      | Slow-acting, spring apply |
| Ground limestone   | Raise pH                   | 5-10 lbs per 100 sq ft     | Takes 2-3 months          |
| Wood ash           | Raise pH, add potassium    | 5 lbs per 100 sq ft max    | Very alkaline, use caution|
| Greensand          | Add potassium, trace mins  | 5-10 lbs per 100 sq ft     | Slow release              |
| Bone meal          | Add phosphorus, calcium    | 3-5 lbs per 100 sq ft      | Slow release              |
| Blood meal         | Quick nitrogen             | 1-2 lbs per 100 sq ft      | Fast-acting, can burn     |

### Raised Bed Soil Mix (Mel's Mix)
- 1/3 peat moss or coconut coir
- 1/3 vermiculite
- 1/3 blended compost (minimum 5 sources)

**Source:** [UMN Extension - Managing Soil and Nutrients](https://extension.umn.edu/how/manage-soil-nutrients)

## Key Calculations for Design Tool

```
# Compost needed for a bed
compost_cubic_feet = bed_length_ft * bed_width_ft * (amendment_depth_inches / 12)
compost_cubic_yards = compost_cubic_feet / 27

# Raised bed soil volume
soil_cubic_feet = length * width * (height_inches / 12)
soil_cubic_yards = soil_cubic_feet / 27

# Approximate soil weight: 1 cubic yard ≈ 2,000 lbs (varies by moisture)
# 1 cubic yard fills approximately 100 sq ft at 3 inches deep
```
